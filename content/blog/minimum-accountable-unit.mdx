The most useful thing I learned at NovaWerk is this: a project doesn't fail at the strategy level. It fails at the *scope* level.

We have a phrase for the unit you should be working in. We call it the **MAU — minimum accountable unit**. Not minimum viable, not minimum lovable — minimum *accountable*. The smallest piece of work where exactly one person knows whether it's done.

## Why "accountable" not "viable"

"Viable" is a marketing word. It asks whether something is good enough to ship to a user. That's the wrong question early on. The right question is: can one person finish this and tell the team, in plain language, whether they finished it?

The minimum viable feature is often a 6-week thing. The minimum accountable unit is almost always a 1- or 2-week thing.

## How to find your MAU

We use four questions, in order:

1. **What's the artifact?** Not the impact, not the goal — the thing. A document, a working button, an interview, a Figma frame.
2. **Who's responsible?** Exactly one name. "The team" is a smell.
3. **What does done look like?** A sentence you can either say yes or no to. "Improved the onboarding" is not done. "Three users completed the new onboarding without help" is.
4. **By when?** A real date, not "this sprint" or "by end of quarter."

If you can answer all four in a 5-minute conversation, you have a MAU. If you can't, you have a project — not a unit. Keep cutting.

## What a project looks like in MAUs

Tinkerlab's "open-source workshop for teens" — a vision sentence — became these MAUs over six weeks:

- Week 1: One curriculum draft owner = Yu, done = peer-reviewed, by Friday.
- Week 1: Three target schools contacted owner = Min, done = first reply received, by Thursday.
- Week 2: Mentor brief written owner = Yu, done = used in one mock pairing call, by Sunday.
- Week 2: Venue booked for first session owner = Min, done = signed agreement, by Thursday.
- Week 3: First session ran owner = full team, done = retro published.

Nobody talked about "shipping the MVP." They talked about whether the venue was booked on Thursday. That's the level you want to operate at.

## What this isn't

This isn't agile. It isn't OKRs. It isn't a substitute for thinking about what your project *is* — that work happens before the MAU step, not in it. Vision still matters. So does the larger problem you're aiming at.

The MAU is just the part where ambition gets cut down to a size that one person can finish before they lose the thread.

That's the part most projects die at. So that's the part worth getting good at.
