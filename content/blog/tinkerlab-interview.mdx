We sat down with three Tinkerlab students between sessions in late March. None of them had touched code six months ago. All three have a project they're now too defensive to abandon.

We've redacted last names. We did not edit their answers.

## You all came to Tinkerlab having never written code. What was the first session like?

**Yuxuan, 13:** I thought there would be a teacher. Like, an adult who would tell me what to do. The adults just kept asking me what I wanted to make. I didn't know how to answer.

**Tian, 14:** I built a button that turned on a light. That was the whole first session. Three hours.

**Riley, 12:** I broke something in the first 20 minutes and I thought I'd be in trouble. My mentor said "good, now we know what doesn't work." I wasn't ready for that.

## What changed for you after a few sessions?

**Yuxuan:** I stopped asking permission. The mentors don't give permission — they ask what I think will happen if I try the thing. That's a different kind of question.

**Tian:** I started bringing problems from home. There's a part of our garage door that doesn't close right. I think I can make a sensor.

**Riley:** I read the documentation. Not because anyone told me to. Because I needed to.

## What surprised you about the mentors?

**Yuxuan:** They don't actually know everything. The first time I saw a mentor Google something for me, I felt like I'd seen behind a curtain.

**Tian:** They write things down. Every session ends with the mentor typing up what we did. I didn't know engineers did that.

**Riley:** My mentor lives in Singapore. We meet on a video call. I didn't think it would work but it does.

## What's the project you're working on now?

**Yuxuan:** A plant moisture sensor that texts my grandmother when her tomatoes need water.

**Tian:** That garage door thing.

**Riley:** I'm writing a script that sorts my mom's photos by which family member is in them. I'm bad at the face recognition part. I'll get it.

## What do you wish more adults understood about programs like this?

**Yuxuan:** When you ask us what we want to make, mean it. Don't redirect us to the thing you wanted us to make.

**Tian:** Show us your own mistakes. That's where I learn.

**Riley:** Don't give us a kit. We can pick the parts ourselves.

---

We left the interview thinking about Riley's last answer. The default for a "kids' coding program" is a curated kit, a known answer, a step-by-step worksheet. Tinkerlab does almost none of that — and what we heard, more than anything, was that the kids notice. They notice when adults treat them as builders rather than students.

It's a small distinction. It's also the whole thing.
