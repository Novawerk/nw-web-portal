The line on our home page reads: *indifference is the only failure*.

I wrote it, and I sometimes wish I hadn't, because it's the kind of phrase that's easy to nod at and hard to live with. You read it on a Thursday at 9am, full of conviction. Then it's a rainy Saturday in February, you've been on three calls that didn't go anywhere, and the thought of opening the project Discord feels like a tax.

The phrase doesn't help you on the Saturday. Nothing does, really. Which makes me think we need to talk about staying — not starting.

## The first burst is easy

The first month of any project is mostly adrenaline. You meet people, you make decisions, you ship a thing that feels significant because it's the first thing. Photos get taken. Posts get written. People who never come back show up to the launch.

This part is great. It's also misleading. Almost any group of motivated people can do this part. The interesting question is what happens between months three and twelve.

## What "staying" actually requires

I've been part of three NovaWerk projects long enough to see the dip. Here's what I've watched help, in rough order of importance:

1. **A rhythm someone else maintains.** A scheduled retro that someone other than you will host. A standing time. A reminder you didn't write yourself. Heroism is a great way to start something and a poor way to keep something going.

2. **A reason to show up that isn't the work.** The people who stay are the ones who, on the worst week, are coming for the *people*, not the project. Have a 10-minute window in every meeting that isn't about the work. Eat dinner together. Send the picture of your cat.

3. **An exit that isn't a betrayal.** People should be able to take a quarter off without it feeling like quitting. If your community treats reduced engagement as a personal failure, you're optimizing for guilt — and guilt is, weirdly, the fastest way to lose someone.

4. **Visible small wins.** Not "we did the thing!" but "I changed one paragraph in the doc this week." Small visible wins keep the rhythm honest when the big wins are slow.

## What I'm still figuring out

I don't know how to keep someone who is genuinely burned out. I don't think I'm supposed to. The best thing I've done in those moments is offered a real off-ramp — say, "you've done a lot, take six months, come back if you want to" — and meant it.

I also don't know how to scale this. Every NovaWerk project that's stayed alive has had at least one person who treated showing up as a personal practice, not a strategic choice. Whether you can teach that, or only recruit for it, is something I'd love to know.

## Why I still believe the line

If indifference is the only failure, then staying — even imperfectly — is already winning. You don't have to be productive on the rainy Saturday. You don't have to feel inspired. You just have to *not have decided this doesn't matter*.

That, I think, we can ask of each other. The rest is just craft.
