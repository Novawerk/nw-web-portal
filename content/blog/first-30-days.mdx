The first 30 days of Tinkerlab broke at least four assumptions I'd held for years. I want to write them down before I rationalize them away.

This isn't a guide. It's a list, written with the embarrassment of someone who's now run a real session and is less sure of what she knew last month.

## 1. We recruited too fast

We opened the volunteer signup on day three. By day ten, we had 41 mentors and one running session. The math was wrong: each mentor needs a real student to be paired with, and we hadn't even decided what a session looked like.

What we'd do differently: keep the mentor list closed until the first session has run, then expand by 30% per cohort, not 300%.

## 2. We let "MVP" mean "fast" instead of "small"

Our first session plan had three components: a curriculum, a mentor flow, and an event format. We tried to ship all three at once. Two of them were broken.

The fix was to admit that the only thing we needed in the first 30 days was *one* component that worked — the in-person session. We could fake the rest until we knew that mattered.

## 3. We didn't write down what "done" looked like

Until session three, I genuinely could not tell you what a successful Tinkerlab Saturday looked like. Every session ended with some version of "well, that was fun." That's a vibe, not a definition.

By session four we had a 6-point checklist (kids leave with code in their own repo, mentor writes a 1-paragraph retro, etc.). Suddenly the team could disagree about what to change.

## 4. We accepted help from people who weren't ready

A non-profit running on volunteer time has a particular failure mode: someone offers help, you say yes, they ghost two weeks later, and now there's a half-finished thing that nobody owns. We let this happen twice in the first 30 days.

The fix isn't being more careful about who you let in — it's being more careful about what they own. Single-tasks, with deadlines, that fail loudly if they're not done.

## 5. We treated "open" as a slogan instead of a constraint

I wrote about this in [Why we build in the open](/blog/why-we-build-in-the-open). Short version: open by default sounds great until you realize it means writing a public retro after every session you'd rather just forget. We almost gave up the practice in week two. Glad we didn't.

## 6. We didn't make space for the mentors to know each other

Every mentor knew us. No mentor knew the other mentors. After a month, the ones who were closest to quitting were the ones who'd never met another mentor in person. Now we run a 30-minute mentor circle before each session — half of them stay for it.

## 7. We measured the wrong things

We were counting attendees. We should have been counting *return* attendees. The first metric flatters you; the second tells you whether the thing is alive.

---

If you're 30 days from launching something, I'd ask you the same things I wish someone had asked me: what's the smallest version that proves anyone wants this? Who specifically? And when you say "done" — done how?

These aren't profound. They're embarrassing. Which, I think, is exactly the kind of thing this blog is for.
